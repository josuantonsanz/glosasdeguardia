Part 2: What You Are Missing

While your list is comprehensive regarding the "Big Names" (Augustine, Bernard, Anselm), you are missing a few specific works that are historically significant for the theology of the Passion in the Latin tradition.

1. Gregory the Great (Gregorius Magnus) – PL 75 & 76

You have listed exegetes, but omitted the single most influential allegorist of the Middle Ages regarding suffering. * Work: Moralia in Job. * Why it fits: Gregory explicitly interprets Job as a "Type" of Christ. Books 1–5 and specifically his commentary on Job’s suffering are the primary source for medieval authors connecting human suffering to the Passion. It is impossible to understand medieval Passion exegesis without the Moralia.

2. Aelred of Rievaulx – PL 195

You have the Victorines and Bernard, but Aelred provides the bridge to late medieval visual meditation. * Work: De Institutione Inclusarum (Rule for Recluses). * Section: Specifically the section De triplici meditatione (Meditation on the Past). * Why it fits: This contains a vivid, imaginative meditation where the reader is told to "stand at the foot of the Cross" and catch the blood in a cup. It is a precursor to the Franciscan visualization methods found in the Vitis Mystica.

3. Sedulius – PL 19

  • Work: Carmen Paschale.
  • Why it fits: Books 4 and 5 cover the Miracles and the Passion. Along with Fortunatus and Lactantius, this is one of the three great Latin poems on the Redemption used throughout the Middle Ages.

4. Peter Damian (Petrus Damiani) – PL 144 & 145

  • Work: De Laude Flagellorum (Epistola 98/Bk 6).
  • Why it fits: Damian introduces the theology of participating in the Passion through physical discipline. This marks a shift from viewing the Passion as a theological concept to a physical imitation (mimesis).

5. Quodvultdeus (often found in Pseudo-Augustine) – PL 40 or 42

  • Work: Sermo de Symbolo or De Cataclysmo.
  • Why it fits: He is famous for the "Devil as the Mousetrap" metaphor (the Cross as the trap, Christ's flesh as the bait) which was central to patristic soteriology before Anselm changed the focus to "Satisfaction."

6. Guigo II the Carthusian – PL 153 (Sometimes listed under Works of Guigo I)

  • Work: Scala Claustralium (The Ladder of Monks).
  • Why it fits: While a method of prayer (Lectio Divina), its application was frequently directed toward the Passion narratives in the 12th century, solidifying the method of reading the Passion: Lectio (Read), Meditatio (Think), Oratio (Pray), Contemplatio (Rest in the mystery).

Summary of Recommendations

If you are building a definitive bibliography: 1. Add Gregory the Great (Moralia) immediately; it is the backbone of allegorical suffering. 2. Add Aelred of Rievaulx to the "Affective" section. 3. Check PL 158/159 carefully: You have Anselm and Pseudo-Anselm well covered, but ensure you distinguish the Orationes (Prayers) of Anselm, which contain very moving, personal prayers to the dying Christ that differ from his theological treatises.

¿Y en volúmenes?

Dividing the extensive corpus of Patristic and Medieval writings on the Passion into seven thematically organized volumes, while incorporating both Greek (Patrologia Graeca - PG) and Latin (Patrologia Latina - PL) authors, allows for a comprehensive yet manageable collection. The "Part 1" and "Part 2" approach for larger categories is a smart way to ensure all significant texts are included without overwhelming any single volume.

Here is a proposed 7-volume structure, designed to be comprehensive, balanced between East and West, and mindful of the 800-page limit per volume. Each volume explores a distinct facet of the Passion, building from foundational theology to affective piety and liturgical expression.

The Voice of the Fathers on the Passion: A Seven-Volume Collection

Volume I: The Necessity and Mystery of the Cross (Theological Foundations – Part 1)

Focus: The fundamental theological arguments for Christ's suffering and death, emphasizing the divine plan, victory over evil, and the intrinsic link between Incarnation and Passion. Content: 1. S. Athanasius (PG): De Incarnatione Verbi (Chapters 19–57) — The quintessential Eastern statement on the necessity of the Incarnation and Cross for humanity's salvation. 2. Tertullianus (PL): De Carne Christi — A foundational Western defense of the physical reality of Christ’s suffering body against Gnostic denials. 3. Quodvultdeus (PL 40 or 42): Sermo de Symbolo or De Cataclysmo (Selections on the "Mousetrap" theory of atonement) — An influential early metaphor for how Christ overcame the devil. 4. S. Iustinus Martyr (PG): Dialogus cum Tryphone Judaeo (Selections demonstrating Old Testament prophecies of the Passion) — Early apologetic arguments for the Crucifixion's foretelling. 5. Barnaba (PG): Epistola Catholica (Selections on the typology of the Cross and sacrifice) — Early Christian typological interpretations. 6. Cyprianus Carthaginensis (PL): Liber de Laude Martyrii — Connects the Passion to the suffering of early martyrs and the ideal of Christian fortitude. 7. Hilarius Pictaviensis (PL 10): De Trinitate (Books 9-10) — Discusses the profound interplay of divine impassibility and human suffering in Christ. 8. S. Leo I Magnus (PL 54): Sermones de Passione Domini (Sermons 52–60) — Definitive Western Christological pronouncements on the divine and human natures in the Passion. Estimated Length: ~600-700 pages.

Volume II: The Logic of Atonement and Redemption (Theological Foundations – Part 2)

Focus: Later, more systematic, and often philosophical or polemical developments in the theology of the Passion, including the satisfaction theory, moral influence, and Byzantine synthesis. Hugo De S. Victore (PL 176): De Sacramentis Christianae Fidei (Selections regarding Redemption) — A comprehensive systematic overview from a major Victorine theologian. 4. Iohannes Damascenus (PG 94): Expositio Accurata Fidei Orthodoxae (Book IV, sections on the Cross and Christ's suffering) — The authoritative Byzantine systematic theology of the Passion. 5.
